Airlines introducing new routes into markets already served by competitors is a common feature of the aviation industry. Given the scale of global air transport and the number of carriers operating worldwide, competition on established routes is almost unavoidable. However, entering an existing market is financially risky and requires detailed analysis rather than simply increasing capacity to demonstrate market presence. If demand forecasts and operational planning are inaccurate, airlines can incur significant losses in a relatively short period of time. Large network carriers often possess a competitive advantage because they operate hub-and-spoke systems that allow them to feed passengers from connecting flights into long-haul or high-demand services. In contrast, smaller carriers and low-cost airlines tend to rely more heavily on point-to-point operations, focusing on direct routes and simplified services. This reflects the broader distinction between traditional legacy airlines and low-cost carriers that has increasingly shaped the aviation sector over recent years.

Passenger expectations also influence route planning decisions. Certain routes, such as New York to London, are viewed as essential nonstop connections due to consistently high demand. However, when travel involves secondary airports or less connected international destinations, passengers are generally more willing to accept indirect itineraries involving transfers or stopovers. Airline route networks are highly dynamic and change throughout the year in response to seasonal demand patterns. Within Europe, for example, airlines often expand operations during the summer season by increasing frequencies or launching temporary routes to leisure destinations. These decisions are driven by teams of network analysts who evaluate large volumes of operational and commercial data to determine whether a route is likely to generate sustainable profit.

Operational feasibility is another critical consideration in network planning. Airlines must account for aircraft availability, crew scheduling, airport congestion, and slot restrictions at major hubs. International operations introduce additional complexity, as airlines must comply with government regulations, bilateral air service agreements, and traffic rights. Entering a new international market therefore requires not only demand forecasting, but also a detailed understanding of regulatory and logistical constraints. Strategic considerations can also influence route decisions. In some cases, airlines may operate routes with relatively weak short-term demand if they believe the service could become commercially valuable over time. Airports and tourism authorities may further encourage route development by offering incentives, marketing support, or financial assistance aimed at stimulating visitor numbers and economic activity.

Route planning is therefore a continuous and adaptive process. Prior to the COVID-19 pandemic, many legacy carriers introduced approximately fifty new routes annually while simultaneously discontinuing a large number of underperforming services. This illustrates the highly competitive and experimental nature of airline network development. Destinations that repeatedly gain and lose routes may struggle to justify long-term investment in tourism promotion, highlighting the importance of sustainable route planning rather than short-term publicity. In the post-pandemic environment, airlines have become increasingly flexible in their planning strategies. Rather than committing to entire seasonal schedules, some carriers now operate shorter peak-season windows, such as mid-June to mid-September, to reduce financial risk while still capturing strong leisure demand. Although this can improve airline efficiency, it may not necessarily help destinations overcome wider seasonality challenges.

Modern route planning increasingly depends on advanced analytical tools and machine learning techniques. Airlines combine historical scheduling data, passenger booking trends, and sentiment analysis to improve forecasting accuracy. Two important components of these systems are the Connection Builder and the Market Share Model. The Connection Builder predicts the number and quality of itineraries available to passengers within a market, while the Market Share Model estimates the attractiveness of competing itineraries using factors such as departure timing, connection duration, airline preference, and routing efficiency. Together, these tools allow airlines to make more informed strategic decisions in an increasingly competitive aviation market.
